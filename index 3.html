Ch. 01

Thermochemistry & Energy

Energy is conserved, never created or destroyed — it just changes form or moves between system and surroundings. Thermochemistry is the bookkeeping of that movement.

The big concepts

  • Enthalpy (H) — the heat content of a system at constant pressure. We can't measure H directly, only the change: ΔH.
  • 1st Law of Thermodynamics — energy is conserved.
  • 2nd Law — entropy of the universe always increases.
  • Exothermic — releases heat. ΔH is negative. Products sit lower on the PE diagram than reactants.
  • Endothermic — absorbs heat. ΔH is positive. Products sit higher than reactants.
  • Catalyst — lowers activation energy (Ea), speeds up the reaction in both directions, does NOT change ΔH.
On a PE diagram: the hill is activation energy. The difference between reactant level and product level is ΔH. A catalyst shrinks the hill but never moves the endpoints.

The formulas

Heat & Specific Heat
q = m · c · ΔT
q = heat (J) · m = mass (g) · c = specific heat (J/g·°C) · ΔT = Tfinal − Tinitial
Hess's Law
ΔHrxn = ΣΔHsteps
Flip a reaction → flip the sign of ΔH. Multiply a reaction → multiply ΔH by the same factor.
Heats of Formation
ΔH°rxn = Σ ΔH°f(products) − Σ ΔH°f(reactants)
ΔH°f of any element in its standard state = 0. Multiply each ΔH°f by its coefficient.
Calorimetry
qlost = − qgained
Heat lost by the hot thing = heat gained by the cold thing. Solve with q = mcΔT on both sides.

Stoichiometry with ΔH

Treat ΔH like a "product" of the reaction. If the equation gives ΔH = −890 kJ for combustion of 1 mol CH4, then burning 2 mol releases 1780 kJ. Use mole ratios from the balanced equation.

Ch. 02

Heating Curves

A graph of temperature vs. heat added. The slopes and the flats tell two completely different stories.

What's happening at each segment

SegmentWhat's happeningEnergy typeFormula
Sloped (going up)Temperature rises within one phaseKinetic ↑q = mcΔT
Flat at melting ptSolid → liquid (fusion)Potential ↑q = n · ΔHfus
Flat at boiling ptLiquid → gas (vaporization)Potential ↑q = n · ΔHvap
During phase changes (the flats), temperature does NOT change. The energy is breaking IMFs, not speeding up particles. KE is constant; PE goes up.

Endothermic vs. exothermic phase changes

Endothermic (absorbs heat)

Melting · Vaporization · Sublimation
Going from more ordered to less ordered.

Exothermic (releases heat)

Freezing · Condensation · Deposition
Going from less ordered to more ordered.

Solving a heating-curve calculation

Break the journey into segments. For each segment, pick the right formula. Add them all up at the end.

  1. Ice warming to 0°C → q = mcΔT (use c for ice)
  2. Ice melting at 0°C → q = n · ΔHfus
  3. Water warming 0°C → 100°C → q = mcΔT (use c for water)
  4. Water boiling at 100°C → q = n · ΔHvap
  5. Steam heating → q = mcΔT (use c for steam)
Ch. 03

Light & the Quantum Atom

Light is a wave (diffraction proved it) AND a particle (photoelectric effect proved it). Electrons follow the same dual rules.

The relationships

Wave equation
c = λ · ν
c = speed of light = 3.00 × 10⁸ m/s · λ = wavelength (m) · ν = frequency (Hz or 1/s)
Photon energy
E = h · ν   or   E = hc / λ
h = Planck's constant = 6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s
Big wavelength = small frequency = small energy. As λ goes DOWN, ν goes UP, E goes UP. They are inversely proportional in pairs.

The EM spectrum (low → high energy)

Radio → Microwave → Infrared → Visible (ROYGBIV) → Ultraviolet → X-ray → Gamma

Within visible light: red has the lowest energy, violet has the highest.

Electron transitions

  • Absorption — electron absorbs a photon of exact energy and jumps UP to a higher energy level.
  • Emission — electron drops DOWN and emits a photon equal to the energy gap.
  • Energy levels are quantized — only specific energies allowed (no in-between).
  • This is why each element has a unique line spectrum (a fingerprint).
Ch. 04

Electron Configuration

Three rules govern where every electron lives. Memorize them — they cover everything from ground-state notation to spotting an excited atom.

The three rules

  1. Aufbau Principle — fill the lowest-energy orbital first.
  2. Pauli Exclusion — max 2 electrons per orbital, with opposite spins.
  3. Hund's Rule — for orbitals of equal energy (e.g., the three p orbitals), put one electron in each before pairing up. Like seats on an empty bus.

Excited state: an electron has jumped to a higher orbital before the lower one is full. e.g. ground 1s² 2s² 2p² vs. excited 1s² 2s¹ 2p³. Total electron count is the same — just rearranged.

Counting unpaired electrons

Draw the orbital notation for the outermost subshell. Count the singles. That's it. Iron [Ar] 4s² 3d⁶ → in 3d you fill [↑↓][↑][↑][↑][↑] → 4 unpaired.

Ch. 05

Periodic Trends

Almost every trend boils down to two things: effective nuclear charge (Zeff) and the number of filled inner shells (shielding).

Effective nuclear charge

Zeff is the net positive pull the outermost electrons actually feel from the nucleus, after inner electrons "shield" some of it.

  • Across a period → Zeff increases (more protons, same shielding). Outer electrons pulled in tighter.
  • Down a group ↓ a whole new shell is added. Shielding dominates.

The four big trends

PropertyAcross period →Down group ↓Why
Atomic radiusDecreasesIncreasesHigher Zeff pulls e⁻ in; new shells push out
Ionization energyIncreasesDecreasesTighter hold = harder to remove
Electron affinityMore negativeLess negativeTighter hold = more eager to grab e⁻
ElectronegativityIncreasesDecreasesSame logic — F is the king

Ions vs. atoms

  • Cation (lost e⁻) → smaller than the parent atom. Often loses an entire shell.
  • Anion (gained e⁻) → larger than the parent atom. More e⁻ repulsion in the same shell.
  • Isoelectronic series (same e⁻ count) — more protons = smaller ion. e.g., O²⁻ > F⁻ > Na⁺ > Mg²⁺ (all have 10 electrons).

Ionization energy: spotting valence electrons

Successive IEs jump massively when you start ripping off core electrons. If IE₃ is reasonable but IE₄ is HUGE, the atom has 3 valence electrons. The big jump = entered the core.

Metals vs. nonmetals

Metals (lower-left)

Low IE, low EN. Easily LOSE electrons → form cations. Reactivity increases going DOWN (alkali metals, Group I).

Nonmetals (upper-right)

High IE, high EN. Easily GAIN electrons → form anions. Reactivity increases going UP (halogens, Group VII).

Group VIII (noble gases) — full shells, essentially zero reactivity.

Ch. 06

Bonding Basics

Bond type is determined by the electronegativity difference (ΔEN) between the two atoms.

Three types of bonds

Bond typeΔEN rangeWhat happens
Nonpolar covalent0 – 0.4Electrons shared equally
Polar covalent0.4 – 1.7Electrons shared unequally (partial charges)
Ionic> 1.7Electrons transferred (full charges)

Lattice energy & Coulomb's Law

Lattice energy = energy needed to separate one mole of an ionic compound into its gas-phase ions. Bigger lattice energy = stronger ionic bond.

Coulomb's Law (proportionality)
E ∝ (q₁ · q₂) / r
q₁, q₂ = ion charges · r = distance between ion centers
For high lattice energy: want BIG charges and SMALL ions. MgO (2+/2−, small ions) crushes NaCl (1+/1−). To compare two compounds, look at charge first, then size.

Covalent bonding

Two atoms share one or more pairs of electrons so that each gets an octet (Group VIII configuration). Single bond = 1 pair shared. Double = 2 pairs. Triple = 3 pairs.

  • More shared pairs → shorter bond, stronger bond.
  • Triple > Double > Single in strength and shortness.
Ch. 07

Lewis Structures & Formal Charge

Lewis structures are a recipe. Follow the steps in order and they always work — even on the weirder ones.

The recipe

  1. Total valence electrons. Sum group numbers for all atoms. Add e⁻ for anion charges, subtract for cation charges.
  2. Pick the central atom. Usually the least electronegative (never H). Carbon usually wins ties.
  3. Connect with single bonds. Each bond uses 2 electrons.
  4. Distribute remaining electrons as lone pairs on outer atoms first (to give them octets), then on the central atom.
  5. If atoms still don't have octets → form double or triple bonds by moving lone pairs in to share.

Special cases

  • H — only wants 2 electrons (duet, not octet).
  • B — content with 6 electrons (incomplete octet).
  • Expanded octet — atoms in period 3+ (P, S, Cl, Xe…) can hold 10 or 12. Have access to d orbitals.
  • Resonance — when you can draw two or more equally valid Lewis structures by moving double bonds around. The real molecule is a hybrid (average) of all of them. Use ↔ arrows.
  • Oxyacids — H is always bonded to O, never to the central atom. e.g., H2SO4: H–O–S(=O)2–O–H.

Formal charge

Formal Charge Formula
FC = (valence e⁻) − (lone pair e⁻) − ½(bonding e⁻)
Or simpler: FC = (valence e⁻) − (lone pair e⁻) − (# of bonds)
Best Lewis structure: formal charges as close to ZERO as possible, AND any negative FC sits on the most electronegative atom. Sum of all FCs = overall charge of the molecule/ion.

VSEPR & Molecular Polarity

Electron groups (bonds + lone pairs) repel each other and arrange themselves as far apart as possible. That arrangement = molecular shape.

The shapes

Lone pairs take up MORE space than bonding pairs — they squeeze the bond angles slightly smaller. H2O is 104.5°, not 109.5°.

Molecular polarity

A molecule is polar if its bond dipoles don't cancel. Two things to check:

  1. Are the bonds polar? (ΔEN check) — if all bonds are nonpolar, molecule is nonpolar.
  2. Is the shape symmetric? — if symmetric AND all outer atoms are identical, the dipoles cancel → nonpolar.
Nonpolar shapes (when symmetric)

Linear · Trigonal planar · Tetrahedral · Trigonal bipyramidal · Octahedral
CO2, BF3, CH4, SF6

Polar shapes (lone pair = asymmetry)

Bent · Trigonal pyramidal
H2O, NH3, SO2

Ch. 09

Intermolecular Forces

Attractions BETWEEN molecules (not within). They determine boiling point, melting point, viscosity, surface tension, and vapor pressure.

Types — weakest to strongest

ForceFound inStrength
London dispersion (LDF)ALL molecules — temporary dipoles from electron motionWeakest, but grows with size/# of electrons
Dipole-dipolePolar molecules (permanent dipoles)Moderate
Hydrogen bondingH bonded directly to N, O, or FStrongest of the three
H-bonding rule of thumb — only counts when H is bonded to N, O, or F. "FON it." HF, H2O, NH3, ethanol — yes. H bonded to C? No.

Property effects

Stronger IMFs cause:

  • Higher boiling point and melting point — more energy to separate molecules.
  • Higher viscosity — molecules stick more, flow less.
  • Higher surface tension — molecules pulled into the bulk tightly.
  • Lower vapor pressure — fewer molecules can escape to the gas phase.

Ranking two molecules

  1. First, check the strongest type of IMF each has (LDF only → dipole-dipole → H-bonding).
  2. If tied, compare molar mass / number of electrons (more = stronger LDF).
  3. For LDF comparisons, also consider shape — long/linear molecules pack closer than spherical ones, so stronger LDF.
Ch. 10

Gases & Gas Laws

Gases are defined by what they DON'T do: don't stick together, don't take a fixed shape, don't have a fixed volume. The math follows.

Kinetic-Molecular Theory (the assumptions)

  1. Gas particles are in constant, random motion.
  2. The volume of the particles themselves is negligible vs. the container.
  3. No attractive or repulsive forces between particles.
  4. Collisions are perfectly elastic (no energy lost).
  5. Average kinetic energy is directly proportional to Kelvin temperature.
Temperature is a measure of AVERAGE kinetic energy. At the same T, two different gases have the same average KE — but the lighter one moves faster (smaller m, bigger v in ½mv²).

Pressure units

Equivalent pressures
1 atm = 760 mmHg = 760 torr = 101.3 kPa = 14.7 psi

The gas laws

Boyle's Law (T, n constant)
P₁V₁ = P₂V₂
Pressure and volume are inversely proportional.
Charles's Law (P, n constant)
V₁ / T₁ = V₂ / T₂
Volume and temperature directly proportional. T MUST be in Kelvin.
Gay-Lussac's Law (V, n constant)
P₁ / T₁ = P₂ / T₂
Combined Gas Law (n constant)
(P₁V₁) / T₁ = (P₂V₂) / T₂
Ideal Gas Law (the one)
PV = nRT
R = 0.0821 L·atm / (mol·K) — match your units. P in atm, V in L, T in K, n in mol.

STP & molar volume

STP = Standard Temperature and Pressure = 0°C (273.15 K) and 1 atm. At STP, 1 mole of any ideal gas = 22.4 L.

Molar mass & density from PV = nRT

Molar mass
M = (m · R · T) / (P · V)
Gas density
d = (P · M) / (R · T)
Heavier gas = denser. Higher P = denser. Higher T = less dense (gas expands).

Dalton's Law of Partial Pressures

Total pressure of a mixture
Ptotal = P₁ + P₂ + P₃ + …
Gas collected over water: the gas is mixed with water vapor. To get the dry gas pressure: Pgas = Patm − Pwater vapor. The water vapor pressure depends on temperature (use a table).
Ch. 11

Acids, Bases & pH

Brønsted-Lowry redefined acids and bases in terms of proton transfer. Forget "produces H⁺ in water" — think "donates a proton."

Brønsted-Lowry definitions

  • Acid = proton (H⁺) donor
  • Base = proton (H⁺) acceptor
  • Every acid-base reaction is a proton transfer.
  • Conjugate pair — two species that differ by exactly one H⁺. The acid loses H⁺ to become its conjugate base; the base gains H⁺ to become its conjugate acid.

Example: NH3 + H2O ⇌ NH4+ + OH−

  • H2O donates H⁺ → acts as acid · its conjugate base is OH⁻
  • NH3 accepts H⁺ → acts as base · its conjugate acid is NH4+

Autoionization of water

Equation
2 H2O ⇌ H3O⁺ + OH⁻
Water's equilibrium constant
Kw = [H3O⁺][OH⁻] = 1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴ at 25°C
Given one concentration, divide Kw by it to get the other.

pH scale

pH and pOH
pH = − log[H3O⁺]
pOH = − log[OH⁻]
pH + pOH = 14
Reverse direction
[H3O⁺] = 10−pH
[OH⁻] = 10−pOH
RangepH[H3O⁺]
Acidic< 7> 1 × 10⁻⁷ M
Neutral= 7= 1 × 10⁻⁷ M
Basic> 7< 1 × 10⁻⁷ M
pH is a log scale — each unit = factor of 10 in [H3O⁺]. pH 3 is 100× more acidic than pH 5, not "a bit" more.
